RelativeDB against XGBoost, on the same rows

One relational dataset, one chronological split at 2018-01-01, the same labels on both sides. XGBoost gets a flat encoded matrix; RelativeDB answers a RelQL query over the graph. Every entity is scored at its own anchor time, and every row whose outcome window closes after the data ends (2018-10-17) is dropped rather than counted as a negative.

Anchored at purchase, before delivery. The strongest driver of a bad review is a late delivery, which has not happened yet, so all three sit near chance. Fine-tuning moved this down here and up on the earlier 2018-05-01 split, so the direction is noise, not an effect.

Next-90-day spend after the first order

How much will this customer spend in the 90 days after their first order?

PREDICT SUM(payments.payment_value) OVER (90 DAYS FOLLOWING)
FROM customers WHERE customers.customer_unique_id IN :ids
SystemMAE traininference
XGBoost11.35 0.7 s0.00 ms
RelativeDB (zero-shot)38.25 —30.49 ms

Fine-tuning is the only thing that closes this gap. Zero-shot is 3.4× XGBoost's error; a trained head gets within 25% of it. This row read 709.64 before a fix: a fine-tuned head is fitted on raw targets, but rt_native.score() was still applying the released head's denormalization value = score × label_sd + label_mu on top, transforming it twice.

96% of customers spend nothing in the window, so MAE is dominated by how tightly a model predicts zero.

Repeat purchase after the first order

Will this customer order again within 90 days?

PREDICT EXISTS(orders.*) OVER (90 DAYS FOLLOWING)
FROM customers WHERE customers.customer_unique_id IN :ids
SystemAUC traininference
XGBoost0.584 0.5 s0.00 ms
RelativeDB (zero-shot)0.630 —31.54 ms

The sparse case, and the clearest gap in the set: every test customer is cold-start with exactly one prior order, so per-customer aggregates carry almost nothing and XGBoost is working from order attributes alone. Zero-shot reads the order's items, payments and seller directly. Fine-tuning lands below chance on a 4%-positive target, which is the same instability seen on the other binary task.

What the sparsity slice shows

Almost every row is cold-start

Olist keys every order to a fresh customer_id; customer_unique_id is the person. Of the 400 test rows per task, 388–400 have no prior order. The customer-level tasks are 100% cold-start, so their numbers are the sparse-data result rather than an average over a mixed population.
